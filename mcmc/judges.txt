Judges: estimating the ideological locations of Supreme Court justices.

This program implements an ideal-point model (similar to the 
Legislators example), estimating both the locations of the justices on a latent ideological dimension, and two parameters specific to each case (corresponding to the item difficulty and item discrimination parameters of a two-parameter IRT model).  The data consist of the decisions of Justices Rehnquist,
Stevens, O’Connor, Scalia, Kennedy, Souter, Thomas, Ginsberg and Bryer, in that order, i = 1, . . . , 9. The decisions are coded 1 for votes with the majority, and 0 for votes against the majority, and NA for abstentions.  Truncated normal sampling is used to implement a probit model (with the observed data supplying upper and lower bounds on the truncation points; see the Turnout example).  Alternative specifications are presented below.

		mu[i,j] <- x[i]*beta[j,1] - beta[j,2];

		ystar[i,j] ~ dnorm(mu[i,j],1)I(lower[i,j],upper[i,j]);
		}
	}

	for (i in 1:9){
		z[i] <- step(mu[i,34])  
	}

		x[i] ~ dnorm(0,1)  ## latent trait
	}

Logit Model:

We could also fit a logit model to these same data, and with priors that put liberals on the "left" and conservatives on the "right".  This model doesn't rely on truncated normal sampling, and so doesn't have to make imputations for the latent data ystar[i,j].  This results in much faster convergence.  In theory, one could also code up a probit model this way (using the probit link instead of the logit link), but my experience is that the probit link in WinBUGS is quite buggy, and that in general, probit models in WinBUGS are much more straightforward to estimate via truncated normal sampling of a latent ystar, if somewhat slower.
